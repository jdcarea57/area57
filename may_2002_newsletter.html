XK Weekend - May 10th/11th/12th

This year Brian and Mary Davis had chosen Dartmoor as the setting for their Spring Weekend. We were based at a very nice hotel near Yelverton, on the edge of Dartmoor, about halfway between Plymouth and Tavistock. Everyone arrives in their own time on Friday and greetings are exchanged with familiar faces who are regulars on these weekends. As usual Dick, Eileen, Eric and Margot and ourselves travelled down together, arriving in lovely sunshine. Two places were arranged for us to visit on Saturday, the first being Morwellham Quay which is the highest navigable point of the River Tamar. Copper and later arsenic ore were both mined in large quantities in the area. Over the years deposits ran out and by 1970 the place was derelict. Today the area has been redeveloped and is an ongoing restoration project to show life as it was in the 19th century. There is a trip into the copper mine on a small train, sometimes in complete darkness. Our time was fairly limited but there is plenty to see there.

It had been arranged for us to visit Castle Drogo in the afternoon which was designed and built in the early 20th century by Edwin Lutyens for a Mr Drewe who was the founder of the Home and Colonial grocery stores. (Not many of you will remember them!). We drove across Dartmoor to get there and of course, what had started as a lovely day developed into a downpour! Castle Drogo is up a long steep lane and apparently at 1000ft it’s garden is the highest owned by the National Trust. I don’t think anyone could say it is an attractive building. It was then back to the hotel to get ready for dinner, which like all the meals we had, was very good.

Sunday morning took us to the outskirts of Plymouth to visit Crownhill Fort which is a series of massive earth ramparts, hidden gun emplacements and tunnels built in the 1860s as a defence against attack from the French. We had a guided tour which left us wondering how long it took the soldiers based there to learn their way around all the tunnels.

After Sunday lunch most people were on their way home, but along with about a dozen others, we had decided to stay one more night, so we had an afternoon free to do what we chose. Being so close we decided we would like to see the sea and Plymouth so rather than take the old cars into the heart of an unfamiliar busy town we asked reception to book a couple of cabs for us, which we then phoned when we were ready to go back to the hotel. It was definitely the best way of doing it.

Monday morning we woke to rain and the forecast of rain all day, so the journey home wasn’t pleasant, to say the least. The weekend though had gone as smoothly as Brian and Mary’s weekends always do, and was very enjoyable.

HAYNES SPRING CLASSIC, 24th March 2002

Report by Simon Waldram

My wife Candy had a teaching inspection coming up so I persuaded Raj from work to be my navigator on the Haynes Spring Classic rally. We left Muswell Hill at 7.00 am and made good time to Prescott Hill climb (just North of Cheltenham) where the rally started: 151 cars ranging in age from a 1948 Allard to a 2001 Porshe 911 Carerra. Amongst the assembled cars was a tasty variety of XK 150s and E types, plenty of Morgans and three Ferraris (a Mondial, a 308 GTS and a 348.) Oh dear we could be seriously slow in comparison!

It was the first opening of Prescott since winter and the start was a little late: proceedings were then held up even more when a couple of over enthusiastic early drivers ran out of road and had to be pulled out of gravel traps. We had a good first run at the hill and put in (for us) a quick time of only just over the magic 60 seconds, but our second run had to be aborted when we were red flagged: a rather competative looking Ford Escort rally car running just ahead of us had broken a half shaft on Pardon hairpin.

We then followed the tulip notes for a pleasant 33 mile run to Lord Bathurst’s Cirencester Park Rally stage. This was a bit of a disappointment as speeds were restricted to 30 mph, but as the route was full of potholes we probably wouldn’t have chosen to drive much faster anyway.

Then more tulip notes for a 23 mile run to Castle Combe Circuit where we were allowed 9 laps. We were having good fun and progressing quite fast without too many other drivers overtaking us, but I guess I didn’t fully appreciate how much work my uprated Coopercraft brakes were doing and on lap 5 as I came over Avon Rise to Quarry Corner at 80 to 90 mph my brake pedal went down to the floor. Not a nice feeling! A few quick pumps of the pedal and we did manage to slow down ok: then a very leisurely return to the pits to let my heart slow down. The problem was boiling brake fluid and slugs of brake fluid vapour in the lines. The remedy was a long lunch break whilst we let everything cool down, and then a quick bleed of the brakes with the help of the attendant RAC service. I guess the moral is to use competition brake fluid if you want do any circuit driving!

We then had a short run to Colerne airfield where the fast boys could make full use of the smooth long straight which must be almost a mile in length. For the first time ever (at least during my ownership) the Daimler topped the ton but we were still cautious about a potential repeat of the brake problem so didn’t try any heroics (unlike the Volvo Amazon behind which seemed to spend most of the time travelling sideways.)

The last road stage was the 40 mile run down to the Haynes museum in Sparkford where we finished with some driving skill tests before having a cream tea and a good look around the museum. If you haven’t been then it’s well worth a visit and there were quite a few new exhibits since my last visit.

I would love to tell you that we won one of the best driver awards, but alas I can’t. Still, it was a thoroughly good day and the Daimler ran like clockwork. With the journey home we covered 383 miles in the day at an average of 21.2 mpg, the latter despite some very lead soled right foot driving.

TREASURE HUNT TRING - WARE, Sunday 14th April

Report by Mick & Jacquie Stubbings

The weather was extremely kind, the sun shone all day, which made the treasure hunt all that more enjoyable.We met John and Loraine at Phil and Angie’s. From Cheshunt we travelled to Tring where we met up with all the others who were taking part and in true Jaguar fashion we started with a very large breakfast, (still it had to last us until dinner).

Breakfast over we received our clue sheets and were given our starting times, the time then was about 11:30. It was planned so that we had a stop off time halfway round, this allowed our cars to rest and us to take on more refreshments and stretch our legs.

In the instructions for the second half there was a note that said ignore the sign saying road unsuitable for cars, a few pot-holes, but all right to use, by the time we got to this part we were right behind John and Loraine, John read the sign stopped re-checked then proceeded, I did the same, but 50 yards on we realised we had made a mistake, TOO LATE! We stopped and as luck would have it another car not in the treasure hunt was behind us, so the girls went back and asked them. They said yes we had gone wrong, in fact we were in a dry river bed that 2 weeks before would have completely covered our car in water. The only way out was to continue straight on and about 1-2 miles further on we would be able to drive out, so on we went, but twice we encountered water which we had to get as far up the river bank as possible, as I was following John I held back waiting to see how deep the water was, just in case. When we managed to get out of the river we could not pick up the trail for the rest of the clues, so knowing where the pub was, that we were finishing at, we decided to head straight there, most of you will also know it, The Feathers at Wadesmill. Here we enjoyed a lovely carvery and I was asked how long had I owned an E-type off-roader.

VINTAGE CARS SHOW THEIR APPEAL - Event helps charity zoom in on target

From The Mercury - May 10th 2002

A £500,000 appeal for a breast cancer unit is on its final lap after a vintage show of classic and bygone cars set the charity's totaliser racing. Cuffley resident Vicki Adkins, chairman of the East and North Herts Breast Unit Appeal, has the target in her sights after a successful vintage and classic car show in aid of the good cause at Stanborough Lakes in Welwyn Garden City on bank holiday Monday. Gleaming machines and finely-tuned engines, provided with the help of Enfield Motor Museum, formed the hub of a day of activities. Stalls, jugglers and a brass band made sure even non-drivers had plenty to admire. The fun day was coupled with an enjoyable evening at Brocket Hall, near Welwyn Garden City. Vicki's appeal to build a dedicated breast cancer unit now stands at £460,000, just £40,000 short of its target.

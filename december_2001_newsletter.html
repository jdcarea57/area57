Newsletter - December 2001

Area Rep’s Christmas Message

You’ll probably find it hard to believe that it’s been a full twelve months since the last Christmas Message... I do !

A lot has gone on again this year; new venues, new friends and yet another first place in the competition for best club stand at the Enfield pageant of Motoring. Well done everyone.

As usual on the food front, both Breakfast Runs were well supported, but this year’s Christmas Dinner looked as if it was going to be a non-runner after a mistake by The George over the booking. True to form, a few of our members came to the rescue and another venue was found with good food in festive surroundings.

Don’t forget to vote next year for the person you think should be the next recipient of the John Dyall Trophy.

Once again well done to everybody, and many thanks for all the support that you have given me and your Area.

HAVE A LOVELY CHRISTMAS AND A VERY HAPPY NEW YEAR !

Alan

Thirty six of us gathered at the Plough, Cattlegate Road on December 8th for our Christmas Dinner. We were looked after well in the conservatory restaurant which we had to ourselves for the evening. It seemed a little quieter than our Christmas dinners have been in the past, maybe the barely audible background music could have gone up a little bit. We did not have the usual noisy bits and pieces that Dick and Eileen usually bring and our two flying balloons didn’t survive very long. On the other hand it may just have been that Eric wasn’t there!! Barbara did her usual good job of reminding people what they had ordered and collecting up the money, and it seemed that a good time was had by all.

By the time this reaches you, we will no doubt have consumed plenty of sausage rolls, mince pies etc. on 17th December, the last meeting of the year.

Anyone needing a breath of fresh air on New Year’s Day could take a drive to the Black Lion at Shenley where the Bentley Club and Vintage Sports Car Club have a gathering from around midday.

If anyone would like a large wine sports shirt with our logo, give Chris Engeham a ring as he has an unused one that he would like to find a home for.

As you know, this year saw Alan and Barbara’s 30th wedding anniversary and Alan’s 50th birthday. Many members of the club decided to club together, and instead of buying a gift, a cheque was presented for them to do with as they pleased. You may be interested to learn that they have spent the money on fitting out their smallest bedroom as an office, in pale green and chrome.

Many of you will know John Mahoney, who was a long time member of Area 57 until his ill health prevented him from taking part in club events.  We have recently heard that after bone marrow tests he has now been pronounced free of leukaemia.  He still has a long way to go, but he says he couldn't have asked for a better Christmas present.  We all wish him well.

THE CAR THAT MONEY CAN’T BUY

The chequered history of the dream machine that still has collectors queuing

From Sunday Express 25th November 2001

WITH its beautiful curves, impeccable heritage and powerful five-litre engine, it attracts hordes of admirers to the Jaguar museum every year. The Jaguar XJ13 is the ultimate dream machine because it is the car that nobody in the world can afford. Now two car enthusiasts have effectively offered blank cheques to get their hands on the famous racer that never made it to the track. One Japanese collector even offered a staggering £7million for the original, with a US rival telling Jaguar to name its price to build him a copy. But Jaguar has refused, saying they have the only version and would like to keep it that way. Anders Clausager, chief archivist at the museum, said: "There is only one XJ13 in existence and we have it here at the museum. We believe there is a replica somewhere in Australia, but this is the real thing. Not surprisingly there are queues of people wanting to see it.

"It was built in 1966 as a prototype, when Jaguar was considering returning to motor racing. It was very advanced for its time and could do 178mph, which in 1966 was pretty good." The XJ13 was the fastest Jaguar around at that time, Clausager believes. Its performance was much better than that of the E-Type, which could reach "only" 150mph. The XJ13 was also the first Jaguar to have its engine housed between the driver and the rear wheels, making it much nimbler than its rivals.

Despite the interest it has attracted from collectors, Jaguar doesn't wish to build a copy. "We cannot see the point in replicas," Mr Clausager says. "It's a free country and, if someone wants to copy a classic Jaguar, we are not going to stop them. We think it's a pointless exercise - but not everyone can afford a real Van Gogh in their dining-room, so I can sort of see their point."

After the prototype XJ13 was built in 1966, Jaguar intended to unveil it in the Le Mans 24-hour race, where it would have left the competition standing in the dust. But the car never had its heyday after the French race organisers changed the rules about engine capacity. The merger between BMC and Jaguar - the move that created British Motor Holdings - also led to the project being mothballed, so the car was kept under wraps. At the time, the company was so worried that news of the car's existence would leak out - and harm sales of the new six-cylinder E-type - that Jaguar boss Sir William Lyons instructed that the car was not to be circuit-tested. But Norman Dewis, then chief development tester for Jaguar, recalled that his boss Bill Heynes suggested the car should be given a run in spite of Sir William's orders. So, early one Sunday morning, the XJ13 was taken to the Motor Industry Research Association test centre near Nuneaton. Mr Dewis clocked up 175mph on the straight with the car lapping at 161.6mph - an unofficial lap record. But Mr Dewis almost paid a hefty price for his curiosity. Sir William summoned him to his office when he heard about the unofficial trial and read the Riot Act. Since Dewis was so enthusiastic about the car, however, he asked what the result of the test had been. And two weeks later, the car returned to the test track for another circuit - this time with Sir William watching.

To check how the car would performed on a real racing circuit, the XJ13 was tested at Silverstone, and performed well against other racing cars. Far more work was needed, though, to turn the car into a Le Mans winner. This would have meant diverting work away from other production cars, so the XJ13 was again shelved. Despite this, many of the lessons learned in developing the XJ13's engine were incorporated into the V12 unit that was introduced into the E-Type in 1972. That link was not lost on the publicity people, and they decided to use the XJ13 in the opening of a film to announce the new V12 E-Type.

But disaster struck during filming. As Mr Dewis reached speeds of 140mph, the tremendous G-forces exerted on the car led to the Jag's rear offside wheel collapsing under the strain. The car flipped end-over-end twice before rolling. Amazingly, Mr Dewis emerged unscathed although the unique car was a complete write-off. Under normal circumstances the car would have been broken up for scrap. But the wreckage stayed in the factory for two years before the company decided to restore it.

The XJ13 now sits in the museum and is one of its biggest attractions. And Mr Dewis, though now in his eighties, still puts the car through its paces at exhibitions. "He is very quick to complain if the car isn't performing well," said Mr Clausager. "He'll tell us how he remembers it being much faster in its heyday and we'll have to give it a tune-up."

Newsletter - July 2001

We had a very good turnout of members at our second July meeting, including Alan, returned from holiday, broken foot, toothache and rehearsals! He almost felt that he needed to re-introduce himself. We also welcomed two new members, Phil and Angie who we had met at Hoddesdon with their Mark IX.

Various people have been to an assortment of shows over the past few weeks; Rotary Show at Chigwell, the Redbridge Show and Matching Green. Hopefully if the nagging from Alan and myself bears fruit, there will be reports of these next time.

The show at Chigwell clashed with the invitation to the SS Register to the Whitewebbs Museum on Sunday 24th June. Eight of our cars were there on a really glorious morning, but unfortunately many of the EDVVS members had chosen to attend a nearby steam and car show. Even more disappointing was the fact that only two visiting SS register members came along. A lovely green SS90 was brought along by Brian Taylor (I hope I have the surname correct), and Nik Drukker was there with his SS 3.5, known to one and all as Myrtle, which was bought new by his father. Despite Nik having mentioned the visit in the Jaguar Driver and his making a couple of phone calls while we were there, no more SS cars appeared. Those of us who have been to the Museum many times can easily take it for granted and it was good to see how impressed Nik was with it. Luckily Dave and Penny Norton from the EDVVS came along with their Buick so he was able to tell Nik and Brian far more about the Museum than we could. The sunshine showed the cars off well and Phil took plenty of pictures. Hopefully, Nik’s will have turned out successfully and we may see good coverage of the visit in a future Jaguar Driver.

Ten of our cars made the annual visit to Hoddesdon Town Centre for the show there on 14th July. We had a different position for the first time. This year we were arranged around the war memorial at the other end of the High Street. I don’t know if this should be regarded as the most prestigious position as the Memorial Site in the Chelsea Flower Show marquee used to be, but for the number of cars we had, it was ideal. Although we had a little shower in the morning, the weather was quite good which I gather was certainly different from many other local areas. As usual, Keith and Audrey provided some after show refreshments in their lovely garden.

On the previous evening a couple of dozen of us went along to a quiz night to raise money for the centre that Dick and Eileen’s son attends. Fish and chips went down well midway through the evening, which was good fun, although some of the questions had us totally confused. One of our tables, however, managed to come third and another one had three winning raffle tickets. The final figures are not known yet, but it is thought that over £1,000 was raised.

An item on the legal section of the Jimmy Young show amused me recently. A woman who had written in was going through divorce proceedings. She was the owner of an E-type which had belonged to her late father. It was in pretty bad condition when she inherited it. Her soon to be ex-husband had spent quite a bit of time and money on its restoration. While not disputing her ownership, he wanted the right to take it out to various car shows. It’s the first time I have heard of access and visitation rights applying to a car before!

Coming up on 5th August is the J.E.C. Show at Blake Hall. The meeting place for this is the Talbot car park on the A414 at 10 a.m. Please ring Alan on if you intend to meet there.

Don’t forget the Steam and Country Show at Holly Hill Farm on Enfield Ridgeway over August Bank Holiday. Our club display will be on the Monday only so please contact Eric if you wish to take part.

No doubt, several of you have received booking forms for the Chigwell Show on 2nd September. Pre-booking is not essential though.

A personal view from Dick Rogers

The weather was good, but where were the cars? Only three SS Register cars arrived, including my Mk V, and my thanks go to the Area 57 members who brought their cars along. New legislation currently being considered in Brussels would make it impossible to buy more than one litre of lubricating oil at a time.

From Classic Car Weekly - Wednesday 11th July 2001

Classic vehicle organisations cautiously await forthcoming legislation which could strictly limit the amount of oil that members of the public can buy, writes Richard Gunn. A draft directive from the European Commission's Directorate General for the Environment is due in the next few months governing the disposal of waste engine oil.  Although the exact contents of the report are unclear at the moment, there are strong indications that a ceiling will be imposed prohibiting the sale of any more than one litre of oil at a time. "The logic behind this is that it would have the effect of limiting private persons to just topping up their oil." said BobTomlins, European Affairs Advisor of the International Historic Vehicle Organisation.

The Commission sees individuals changing their oil as a serious source of pollution, with waste oil being tipped into drains etc."  Although it is already illegal to dispose of oil in such a way in this country, there is little the authorities can do to enforce the rule, as it is a simple matter to throw waste material away in secret with little fear of being caught.  With most engines requiring at least three litres of oil, the legislation could all but bring about an end to this aspect of routine home servicing of classic cars.

However, there are fears that the directive could go even further, "A second likely response to the problem of tipping waste away could be to limit the sale of oil to private individuals only when they have used oil in exchange, as I believe has recently been introduced in the Netherlands," said Bob "This would also mean that oil could only be sold by licensed outlets that have facilities to handle the waste oil." "My belief is that, if this were to happen, it would result in the number of places where you could buy oil in reasonable quantities reducing considerably. This would likely have a very severe effect on smaller and specialist suppliers, where you usually find specialist oils, such as those needed or preferred by many historic vehicle enthusiasts. Bob is optimistic, however, that any plans in the report affecting the rights of classic car owners can be changed before the legislation is passed. "When the draft directive is published," he said, "we will know how big a threat we are facing and we can plan our response and tactics accordingly.  Based on the support from the European Parliament that we were able to secure over the End of Life Vehicles Directive, I am reasonably confident that if the Commission were to come up with some ridiculous proposals that seriously impacted on our interests, then we should be able to change them in the directive's progress through the European Parliament"

The legislation would also affect bigger retailers like Halfords, who could see their lubricant profits slashed.  At the moment, the company sells 125 million gallons of oil a year to the public through its 408 nationwide stores. A spokesman for the company said "Oil is an environmentally sensitive area, which is one of the reasons why Halfords has set up oil banks for used oil.  If the legislation comes in, we will adhere to it, but we aren't aware of any impending legislation at the moment."

Newsletter - June 2002

The first item this month should give you all food for thought. After doing the job as Area Rep for eleven years, Alan Collins has decided that he will step down at the A.G.M. in March next year. He has made it known now, so that everyone has plenty of time to think whether it is something they feel they can take on. If there is someone that you feel would be good at the job, ask them if they would be prepared to stand . It is no use nominating someone at the A.G.M. if it is the first they have heard about it. As Area Rep it is essential to have an answerphone and mobile as well as ordinary phone, and the ability to deal with all manner of enquiries. Alan took over from Chris Engeham who was Area Rep for the first five years, so it is understandable that he feels it is now time for someone else to take over. Do give it serious thought and don’t just brush it aside till just before the A.G.M.

ENFIELD PAGEANT - JUBILEE WEEKEND

Another successful and enjoyable Pageant again this year with twenty four cars on display on Sunday and twenty one on Monday. In addition, Phil and Heather Kendon’s Mk VII was in the Jubilee Marquee group, winning a specially commissioned boxed set of the State Landau and horses. In total we showed twenty nine different cars over the two days which shows just how many people were prepared to spend two days there with their cars. For the first time ever, we had an XK140 in our ranks, courtesy of David Ashton, who has now become a new member. On Monday we were joined by Will Plair from Essex with his Proteus C-type replica which generated a lot of interest. The Pageant weekend, more than any other time, is when we really do make a team effort for the Club, starting with Eric who organises all the passes with the Museum. As last year John W. cut the grass on our plot for us. I think Doug may have borne the brunt of car arranging but that and setting up the regalia is always a team effort. The Pageant would not be the same without the bacon rolls. Thanks to Barbara for all the shopping, and bacon frying, plus assistants, Heather, Eileen and myself. If there is anyone else who I should have given an individual mention to, forgive me, but as I say the whole weekend is a great team effort. I think putting on a good display is our main intention but the £50 runners up prize was also welcome.

Alan has passed on details of a show called Blasts From the Past Festival on 10th/11th August at Great Saling (between Great Dunmow and Braintree). It is a car show plus traditional funfair with strongest man contest, tug ‘o’ war etc. Several people have got entry forms and entries do not have to be in until August 1st. We don’t seem to attend so many shows as a club as we used to; maybe this one might be worth a thought.

ENTENTE CORDIALE BURGUNDY TOUR 2002 - report by Loraine & John Clark

John and myself collected my Mum and Edwin and made our way through the rain and spray on the roads from London to Dover. On the approach to the Docks on first catching a glimpse of the sea I gasped – all the others asked what was wrong? I said ‘the sea is very rough’ (I wanted to turn back home). We parked at the harbour side where there was already a display of lots of the Jaguars. As it was still raining we all met in the Churchill hotel where some Ententes had stayed the previous evening, this included Phil and Angie. Phil kindly pointed me in the right direction to collect the route book which has detailed road directions for each day’s tour. Considering the state of the sea the ferry crossing was pretty calm. No excuse to have a brandy or two – sorry John!

On arrival at Calais we headed for the motorway on which we covered some 128 kilometres (we are Europeans so no miles in this write up I’m afraid). After certain parts of the routes we had to zero the trip and I think John was doing this in his sleep. We then went through various towns and villages before arriving at Compiegne. We were then met at the Imperial Theatre by the Mayoress who gave a brief talk which unfortunately was difficult to hear. We enjoyed several glasses of decent champagne and snack biscuits. They had also made sandwiches from a loaf of bread which had been carefully placed back inside the crust of the bread – fascinating.

On arrival at our hotel on Friday evening we were informed by the receptionist that they had given our bedroom to a ‘Mrs Winter’. Well I’m not surprised she didn’t want the room as the bed was so hard – a great wooden board under the mattress, that we hardly slept all night. Thank you Mrs Winter, we’ll do the same for you some day. On Saturday we left Compiegne for our onward journey to Beaune in the Burgundy region. To help to make the journey fun there was a treasure hunt included in the route and I busily read out the clues which were set in rhyme – most were fairly clear but a few you had to think twice about. We stopped at Joigny, a garrison town, where they had a market where I bought a nice bag which I have been using ever since. We stopped for lunch just outside Joigny. Sainte Madeleine Sophie Barat was born in Joigny in 1779 and she was the founder of the Sacred Heart congregation dedicated to the education of girls. In the afternoon we arrived at Pascal Bouchard’s for Chablis wine tasting – I’m afraid it was not to our liking so we declined the buying. On arriving in Beaune we found our hotel, ‘The Panorama’ and the view from our rooms was of vast vineyards. There was a swimming pool but it was outdoors so none of us braved it – although mainly warm and dry there was a strong wind.

Sunday was not a day of rest – no driving but lots to see and do in town by foot. Whilst the Ententes left their cars in a guarded car park so the French could enjoy looking at them we went into town where we visited the Hotel Dieu (Hospices de Beaune). This was a hospice used many years ago for people who were unwell and apparently, in times of epidemic, two had to share each bed. The outstanding feature of this building is it’s amazing multi-coloured and patterned roofs. We were then escorted around the centre of Beaune by a tour guide. After that we went to Patriarche winery where we were all given tastevins to which we attached our red, white and blue ribbons. Down in the cellars by candlelight we used our tastevins to sample both white and red wines. Only one or two were to our liking so we only bought a couple of bottles. Phil and Angie bought a bottle of blackcurrant cassis.

Monday was a visit to Autun but on the way we stopped at Château de Sully which is said to have the most beautiful courtyard of any Château in France. The Château was the birthplace of Field Marshal MacMahon, 1st Duke of Magenta, who was President of the French Republic in 1873.On arrival in Autun we all made our way to the Theatre where we were given a talk by the Deputy Mayor of Autun. The theatre was very ornate. Autun city was founded by Emperor Augustus as a prestige Roman town to honour the local Aedus tribe. The city’s position on the great commercial and military road between Lyon and Boulogne bought it great wealth and prosperity. There was a fantastic shop in town which sold a variety of hardware and where I could not resist buying a ceramic olive oil holder/bottle.

On the return trip to Beaune we stopped at a couple of monuments: Temple Janus – two walls of a square tower were probably the remains of the Sanctuary of the Temple; Portre St Andre – one of the survivors of 4 original gates in the Gallo-Roman fortifications and Pyramid Pierre Couhard which dates back to the 1st century AD and was either a funeral monument or a cenotaph come tomb. On arrival at the pyramid there were a couple of youths banging drums.

Tuesday was the Cote de Nuits tour. We drove to Château du Savigny les Beaune where the Jags parked all around the Château and in its grounds. The Château was built in the 14th century by Marechal de Bourgogne and was restored in the 17th century. The interior was restored more recently to accommodate receptions and Gala dinners.

The Château houses four vehicle museums, namely The Arbath collection of 30 different models of endurance cars, the model aviation collection with over 650 small scale models, the motorcycle collection of 500 exhibits dating back to 1903 and a collection of 60 jet fighter planes – two of which were Jaguars and one of which had served in the Gulf war.

We carried on our journey for 32 kilometres before reaching Nuits St George which is famous for its fine wine. After another 20 kilometres we stopped to visit Château Clos de Vougeot which was built in 1551 by Dom Loisier, the 48th abbot of the Cistercian community. We saw four huge wine presses built by the monks of Citeax; former wineries; a statue of the basket carrier sculpted by Henri Bouchard; a large storeroom; a 28metre well dug into the rock near the former kitchens and a collection of old tasting cups. In 1944 the Château was bought by the Confriere de Chevalier Du Tastevin (Brotherhood of the Knights of the Tastevin) of which we watched a short video. Coaches picked up respective parties in the evening and took us to the Château du Savigny les Beaune for our Gala dinner. The rooms seated hundreds of us and although the service was good the same could not be said for the 5-course meal and we may not bother booking up for it next year.

Wednesday was the start of our first leg home – Beaune to Compiegne. We arrived at our hotel to discover we had been allocated the same room as previously, remember the ‘hard bed’ so I spoke to the lady in charge and she had no choice but to offer us another room – I can tell you the beds were soft and we had a good night’s sleep – I told you we would get our own back on Mrs Winter. At the recommendation of Bob and Jan who were also on the tour we booked to eat in the hotel restaurant that evening as John had already covered 393 kilometres during the day and not forgetting my navigating. The meal was the best all holiday – very enjoyable and tasty – with a variety between us of duck, chicken and salmon.

Thursday was the start of our last leg home to Calais and onwards. We went on back roads through Noyon and Villers Carbonnel where we had to constantly overtake big lorries, before reaching Arras where all the Jags parked in the Grande Place. There was a Porsche club already in the Place but their mere 19 exhibits were overshadowed by our 170 Jags. John briefly met John Maries of Jaguar Cars who was presenting the men with Jaguar tie pins. John M’s jag was a two-tone long wheel base Daimler Six – very impressive and roomy. You know what car we want next! We stopped for lunch at the same café as Phil, Angie and some of their friends. John being as helpful as ever assisted Angie by finishing off half a cow and chips. John had ordered a grand beer which was so large I could not even pick the glass up with one hand – he had to share it as he was driving – what a shame. I had a lovely avocado and crab salad and Mum and Edwin just had a drink. After lunch Mum and I bought some fresh bread to take home and then John chose some smelly cheeses as well in another shop. Back in the Daimler we headed for the motorway to take us the last stretch of the tour to Calais car ferry port. On arriving exactly on time at 4pm for the 5pm crossing we were told the ferry would be delayed by 20 minutes because it was windy.

Several of us got out of our cars and had a good gossip until it was time to load on to the ferry. The crossing wasn’t as good as on the way out – a lot of vibration and noise especially when we were held outside Dover for a while. We did a minimum of 1,900 kilometres over the holiday and thoroughly enjoyed every minute of it and we are looking forward to next year.

Newsletter - October 2003

BREAKFAST RUN - 5th October 2003

There is a possibility that this will be the last report of a Breakfast Run to Rickling Green as Barbara was told by his son Sean, that Tim Proctor has received a very good offer for The Cricketers. No doubt we will find out more in the New Year but there is no doubt that our Breakfast Runs will continue to another, as yet unknown, venue if necessary. On October 5th, the good weather that we have enjoyed for so long, continued and we had a lovely sunny morning; a bit cold for some people! Several people have commented on how nice the breakfast was this time and also how promptly everything was served. As usual several of us returned to Alan and Barbara’s to drink more tea and coffee and eat cake and biscuits.

SUSSEX SEA-ESTA - Friday 24th to Sunday 26th October

Report by Audrey Williams

Once again a superb weekend organised by Whitewebbs Museum. We travelled to Alfriston (near Eastbourne) in brilliant sunshine on Friday afternoon. Parked in the reserved area set aside especially for the Club, checked in and had enough time to explore the village and shops & visit a tea-room before it got dark.

Saturday was another glorious but cold day after a heavy frost. We travelled in convoy (20+ vehicles) to Bentley wildfowl and motor museum. A colleague from Whitewebbs accompanied us on our tour around the extensive wildfowl collection. Luckily Chas was very knowledgable about the various swans, ducks & geese from all over the world so it made it particularly interesting for us.

The motor museum was an incredible private collection of rare cars including a :-

1909 Alldays & Onions 10/12 HP, 1937 Bentley James Young (with cantilever doors) ,1906 Brasier 5 seater tourer, 1904 Darracq, 1903 De Dion Bouton, 1928 Minerva Type AK 32/34 HP, and a 1965 Trabant

All amazing !

We had the afternoon to ourselves so Keith and I had a look around Eastbourne and Beachy Head. On the Sunday we visited Filching Manor & motor museum. The Manor House built in 1450 was very beautiful, but the museum was more of a huge workshop, with several vehicles in various stages of restoration. It was interesting to see two veteran cars being prepared for the London to Brighton run on Sunday November 2nd. Malcolm Campbell's Bluebird K3 was also on display. Sadly the family who owned Filching had only the previous week lost their father, so it was purely the family's kindness in honouring their booking that made our visit possible.

As on previous occasions there was a quiz after the meal in the evenings. The topic this time was Football. Keith and I surprised ourselves by only having two missing answers out of 50! - as we know very little about sport (it must have been easy!) Don't be surprised to find the questions recycled for one of our Club's weekends away!!

As there is very little to write about this month, I am taking the opportunity to include an article about nostalgia that will ring true to lots of you. It was passed to me by someone who found it on the Internet and I have heard a similar version on the radio, so it may be familiar to some of you already.

According to today’s regulators and bureaucrats, those of us who were kids in the 50's, 60's, and 70's probably shouldn't have survived.

Our baby cots were covered with brightly coloured lead-based paint which was promptly chewed and licked.

We had no childproof lids on medicine bottles, or latches on doors or cabinets and it was fine to play with pans.

When we rode our bikes, we wore no helmets, just flip flops and fluorescent 'clackers' on our wheels.

As children, we would ride in cars with no seat belts or air bags. Riding in the passenger seat was a treat.

We drank water from the garden hose and not from a bottle - tasted the same.

We ate dripping sandwiches, bread and butter pudding and drank fizzy pop with sugar in it, but we were never overweight because we were always outside playing and not sitting in front of a computer/Playstation.

We shared one drink with four friends, from one bottle or can and no one actually died from this.

We would spend hours building go-carts out of scraps and then went top speed down the hill, only to find out we forgot the brakes. After running into stinging nettles a few times, we learned to solve the problem.
We would leave home in the morning and play all day, as long as we were back before it got dark. No one was able to reach us all day and no one minded.

We did not have Playstations or X-Boxes, no video games at all. No 99 channels on TV, no videotape movies, no surround sound, no mobile phones, no personal computers, no Internet chat rooms. We had friends - we went outside and found them.

We played conkers and street rounders, and sometimes that ball really hurt.

We fell out of trees, got cut and broke bones and teeth, and there were no lawsuits. They were accidents. We learnt not to do the same thing again.

We had fights, punched each other hard and got black and blue - we learned to get over it.

We walked to friend's homes.

We made up games with sticks and tennis balls and ate live stuff, and although we were told it would happen, we did not have very many eyes out, nor did the live stuff live inside us forever.

We rode bikes in packs of 7 and wore our coats by only the hood.

Our actions were our own. Consequences were expected.

The idea of a parent bailing us out if we broke a law was unheard of. They actually sided with the law. Imagine that!

This generation has produced some of the best risk-takers and problem solvers and inventors, ever. The past 50 years have been an explosion of innovation and new ideas.

We had freedom, failure, success and responsibility, and we learned how to deal with it all.

And you're one of them. Congratulations!

We have had the luck to grow up as real kids, before lawyers and government regulated our lives, 'for our own good '.

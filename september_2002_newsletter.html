Newsletter - September 2002

I can imagine that when those people who attended the Holly Hill Show on August Bank Holiday, received last month’s Newsletter mentioning a good forecast, there were some ribald comments. As it turned out it was a chilly, miserable day and those who had bravely turned up in shorts soon changed their minds. We told Loraine Clark that she looked like Nanook of the North. Now in it’s second year, the show seems to be getting good support. The combination of heavy horses, steam and classic cars does mean that there is plenty of interest. There was a small fair, mainly children’s rides but we had a go on the dodgems of course. Unfortunately they didn’t compare very favourably with the ones at Great Saling. We put on a display of nineteen cars and were later joined by an Austin Swallow.

In complete contrast, the Chigwell Show six days later enjoyed absolutely glorious weather. Phil Kendon had asked the organisers if we could have a larger space than last year, which had been so cramped. We were given an embarrassingly large area on which we made a semi-circle of our ten cars. With such lovely weather it was a very pleasant day, but there is no doubt that this show is not what it was. It seems as though the fairground is getting larger every year. The amount of non M.G. cars has got smaller; there are less stalls of interest and the arena events are not what they were. The craft tents seem to have less in them and the country show aspect of having judged flower and vegetable, flower arrangements and handicraft competitions has disappeared altogether. While some aspects of the show’s decline can be put down to the fact that it is not the Metropolitan Police any more, much of it goes back further than their withdrawal. A friend of ours who has recently got involved with M.G.Bs attended for the first time and was surprised at the small amount of spares available at an M.G. show. As usual, the M.G. club sent over a judge to choose a favourite Jaguar. The ten year old daughter of Jan who does all the microphone work on the day, chose Phil and Heather’s MK VII. Apparently this little girl has lots of Jaguar books and is not interested in her dad’s M.G. She appeared to take her job seriously, walking around with a clipboard and writing things down.

I gather that the Hoddesdon style show at Buntingford on 7th September attracted about a hundred cars, but the East Anglian Day, at Stowmarket the next day, wasn’t very well supported.

On Friday 13th, a dozen of us headed off to Hampshire to the Botley Park Hotel, which is roughly half way between Winchester and Portsmouth. It was nearly a weekend of trains and boats and planes. We had the first two but the nearest we got to planes was sitting chatting to a Harrods helicopter pilot. Apparently a business man had chartered the flight using the landing area at the hotel and then going to the Southampton Boat show. The weather was glorious, so we just sat outside till it was time to get ready for dinner. It has now become traditional on these weekends to have quizzes or games in the evening. On Friday night, Keith and Audrey Williams tested our nursery rhyme memories and I had organised some pictures of famous faces to be recognised. Quite a lot of frustration for people who couldn’t bring names to mind.

We had hoped to go into Portsmouth on Saturday to see the Mary Rose, Victory or any of the other numerous places of interest. However, it turned out to be a special heritage weekend and everything needs pre-booking. Instead following the recommendation of friends of Tom and Enid Jones, we drove to Hamble and after a leisurely walk around the little village and a lunch break, we took a river trip along the Hamble river, passing millions and millions of pounds worth of boats moored all along. This was the area where the TV programme, Howards Way was filmed and the boat turned round at the riverside pub used in the series.

When we got back to the hotel a game of petanque was agreed on, with girls taking on boys. Enid decided to go for a rest, so Tom had split personality playing for both sides. The girls won!

After dinner that evening, Keith and Audrey tested some of our English knowledge, plurals, word meanings etc. from an old school book which was probably published in the forties or fifties. We had taken a game called Pass the Bomb which caused a great deal of noisy enjoyment. The next morning the boys took their revenge and beat the girls at carpet bowls. As this was played on bumpy grass it did tend to cancel out the effect of the bias of the bowl (and any skill of the players!)

We said good bye to Tom and Enid who were heading home, and the rest of us went to Alton and a trip on the Watercress Line Steam Railway, a ten mile stretch using full size locomotives. Time for home then and the delights of the M25; Phil and Heather’s direction towards the Dartford Crossing being much better than the way the rest of us were going, with the usual crawl from junction 10 to past 16.

As usual, good company, pleasant surroundings and very good weather meant another enjoyable weekend.

Quite a few of us went to the Quiz Night on 6th September which was raising funds for the centre attended by Dick and Eileen Rogers' son. At an enjoyable evening with a fish and chip supper, the sum of £950 was raised.

MORRIS MINOR CLUB CAPEL MANOR SHOW - Sunday 8th September

Report by Dick Rogers

It was a fine day, and I arrived at about 11:30. All the Morris Minors were parked in a separate field, so I parked along with a Daimler 250 V8 and a Series 1 XJ. There were some interesting pre-war cars on display, and also a lot of cars from Whitewebbs Museum were in attendance. A 1930s Austin won the prize for the best older car, and a 1960s Vauxhall Victor got the award in the Classic Car section.

I departed at about 4 o’clock and the weather turned wet about an hour later, but by then the car was back in the garage. (That was lucky, wasn’t it John?)

Newsletter - January 2005
 

Some of you may receive a subscription renewal reminder this month. Please remember that, after discussion last year, it was decided that renewals received after the end of February will incur an additional £5 rejoining fee.

Just a brief mention of the Christmas Dinner at the Old Manor, last month. It seemed to be a popular venue again although being a little nearer Christmas, parking was more difficult due to the pantomime at the Wyllyots Centre. Thanks to Barbara for her usual organisation and Loraine for printing out our menu choices again.

We went to the New Year Gathering at Shenley which we found a little disappointing. Compared to the numbers of cars that used to be a the Cock O’ the North, the turnout was low and with ordinary cars parked with the "oldies" space was at a premium. On the same day, Eric went to a meeting at Rickmansworth before going to Shenley, where again I believe I’m right in saying that vehicles were a little limited but there was an interesting local museum to visit.

Don’t forget that the A.G.M. is approaching (21st March) and we will be awarding the John Dyall Trophy again for services to the Club. If anyone out there would like to take a more active part in the running of the Area or has any suggestions for its improvement, please speak to Alan.

Double Trouble or Hat Trick?

On Tuesday 18th January Ron Risk took Pam to hospital in Sawbridgeworth for a hip replacement operation. Wednesday he took Brian Kingsley to Harefield for heart surgery. Thursday he complained of pain in his left side. Friday he went to the doctor's, who sent him to hospital. Sunday he had an operation for a twisted hernia! The whole gang in different hospitals in different parts of the country! Ron and Pam were both returned home on Tuesday and Brian was moved from Harefield to the QE2 at Stevenage on Friday 28th which is a better location for most visitors. He was feeling quite down on Friday, so on Saturday Sally, Len, Ron and Pam visited him at the QE2 and spent a couple of very entertaining hours comparing scars, injections, enemas and flatulance! We were all amazed at Brian's recovery. He was dressed and walking about and looked very well indeed. Incredible after such a major operation! Only draw back he is not allowed to drive for 6 weeks, Ron not allowed to drive for 3 weeks and Pam for 3 months, but we're all looking forward to making the breakfast run. We would like to thank Len & Sally and Keith Williams for visiting and transport, and Wendy & Doug and Alan for their best wishes. If you can spare the time, Brian would welcome a phone call or a visit. QE2 Hospital, Datchworth Ward. Telephone 01707-365019 Ext. 4245 or 4247.

Why I’ve still got that lovin’ feline...

By Bill Bradshaw - Sports Editor (The owner of two Jaguars)

Daily Express Jan 12th 2005

It's a love affair, my relationship with Jaguar, and like the vast majority of others, it begins with looks. They have just brought out their latest sports car, the stunning X150 but, for me, it started way back in the Sixties when I was a kid collecting Dinky and Corgi cars. The pride and joy of my collection was a red Jaguar Mark I 2... a police car version. It fired my imagination because the flickering black and white TV programmes of my primary school years were filled with Jaguars, either crammed with villains fleeing a bank job or the stylish cops trying to foil them. No Hiding Place, Gideon's Way, even The Saint seemed packed with them. I loved the racy, curved styling and the oval grille that looked both beautiful and menacing at the same time. The Mark 2 boasted lavish chrome, the fashion at the time, but was also cosseted with acres of walnut and leather. I knew my father, an insurance man on the up at the time, coveted one. He made do in those days with a Ford Zephyr (more American, rather more vulgar) and then later, trying to redress the balance, a big Wolseley 6/110 ... similar amounts of cowhide and forestry sacrificed but much too staid. I promised myself that one day... one day... I'd have a Jaguar. A Mark 2. My mind was made up. By the time I'd passed my test in the mid-Seventies, the world had changed. The Middle East oil crisis was hitting motorists with massive rises in fuel prices (sound familiar?) and few car makers were harder hit than Jaguar. Back then, it made only big cars: big, fast, and gas guzzling. The fantastic E-type had to go. The Mark 2 had long gone, phased out in 1969, and in its place sat the XJ6, still highly desirable and much more "modern" but not quite so alluring to me. And I could not afford its bonnet, never mind the whole car. And so my early car-owning years were filled with Riley Elfs, Ford Consul-Capris and Hillman Avengers. Oh dear. Would I ever afford a Jag? Hardly: I once bought a Triumph 2000 (wood and leather again) but flogged it the same week because I couldn't afford the insurance. My brother-in-law bought an XJ6. He was six years older and much wealthier than me. How I admired him. Maroon with beige leather, it was a beauty. I could still only dream but he allowed me the odd drive. Like John "two Jags" Prescott, I was smitten.

Yet I also feared the costs of servicing and repairs, even if I could afford one. So the Eighties brought me Honda Preludes and, my first classic, a "Saint" Volvo 1800. But how I still wanted a Jag. Eventually, I took the plunge. I risked a seven-year-old Jaguar XJ40. Metallic green with full beige leather trim. I was earning a few more bob and had a company car as back-up now. The Rover went and my "new" Jaguar arrived. After more than 20 years, my love affair was consummated. It did not let me down. What about the poor reliability I feared? Never missed a heartbeat. And they do live and breathe, you know. The Jag -steam locomotives apart - is the most animated machine with a wonderful growl under the bonnet and its own personality. Two years later came a sportier, beefier, XJR and then, after browsing through a car-mart magazine, I saw the ad I could not pull myself away from.

The Mark 2. Actually, it's a 1967 Daimler V8 250 but it's the Mark 2 all right. Same body, same wood, same leather. The mechanicals differ but it is the same beast -with an even prettier oval grille. I bought it. It caused ructions at home but I loved it - and still do. I was under orders that my "everyday" XJR had to go, with a sensible car to replace it. Something practical... with more miles to the gallon. What did I do? I imported a Nissan-based Jaguar Mark 2 lookalike from Japan. A little cutie called a Mitsuoka to give me nearly 50mpg. Mad? Very likely but Jaguars do that to you. I've still got the Mark 2 but was happy to flog the lookalike in favour of the splendid new S-type, introduced six years ago, which is the modern Jag paying homage to the Mark 2. Retro curves and the same oval grille but with all mod cons: ABS, CD, air-con, electric everything, the whole works. And wood. And leather. They may be owned by Ford now; they may have the rather less attractive X-type on the model list these days, but they are still Jags. And I'm still in love.

We had our third Breakfast Run of the season on 8th October. It was also the third time that we had been to the Woodman at Nuthampstead, having held our last two summer runs there. It was a beautiful sunny morning so an early drive on quiet roads was a pleasure. Our nephew Graham lives in Reed, the next village, so we arranged for him and his friend Helen to join us. While getting ready to bring his 1930’s Triumph Gloria, he was surprised to see Alan and Barbara followed by Graham and Karen. He did wonder whether we were all having a scenic ride but the reason was the wrong turning taken by Alan and Barbara. I am assured that it was driver error, not the navigating. We all enjoyed a very good breakfast although our numbers were down a bit. As I said last month, it would be a great pity to stop this traditional start and end to the season.

Don’t forget to give your names to Barbara for the Christmas Dinner on 2nd December at Pearse House, the same venue as last year. She will require a deposit of £5 per head.

Keith W. passed me an article recently in which The Times motoring correspondent had been given the rare chance to drive the only Jaguar XJ13 since the JDHT had completely restored it in the summer. It had been designed to compete at Le Mans in the mid sixties. The 502 bhp, 5 litre V12 engine was ready in 1964 but by the time the car was ready two years later it had already been outpaced by the 7 litre Ford GT40 and the XJ13 was never entered. Nobody really knows the value of this one off car, but ten years ago the JDHT turned down an offer of £7 million. The beautiful car was the work of Malcolm Sayer, the man behind the C, D, and E-types. Norman Dewis, the former chief test driver for Jaguar had a near fatal accident with the car in 1971 while filming a promotional video, when a wheel collapsed at 145 mph. It flipped , somersaulted and barrel-rolled into a field, but Dewis, a small man, was able to retreat under the dashboard and wasn’t hurt.

Andrew Frankel writes that although the experience of driving such a rarity at Gaydon track was nerve-racking, it was not a difficult car to drive as Jaguar had been well aware that winning a race like Le Mans didn’t depend just on the car, but not tiring the drivers. The full syncromesh gearbox will not allow you to miss out gears, the clutch is as gentle as a road car and the engine for all it’s ferocity idles cleanly and pulls away smoothly. The acceleration is savage even when using a fraction of the revs it would have pulled in it’s heyday. It is lighter than the cheapest Ford Focus but has more power than a new Ferrari F430.

If there is a bright side to the XJ13’s status as the greatest "what if" in British motor racing history, it is that one of the most beautiful and fascinating cars ever built was never written off on the race track. And if you are incredibly lucky and very, very careful it can still be driven to this day. Just check your insurance beforehand!

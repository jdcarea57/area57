Newsletter - September 2006
 
The season is nearly at an end so don’t forget to book for the Breakfast Run on 8th October at the Woodman, Nuthampstead. Barbara is taking names. Over the years our Breakfast Runs have been, and still are, very enjoyable. However, the numbers have dropped in comparison to earlier years, and there must be a point at which it is uneconomic for pubs to lay on the staff needed. So if you used to do these runs or haven’t done one before, why don’t you join us on October 8th. The meeting points are at 8 a.m at the Royal Forest Hotel, Chingford or opposite MacDonalds in Bullmoor Lane, Enfield.

Barbara is also taking names for the Christmas Dinner at Pearse House on 2nd December; details of menu and price will be in a later newsletter.

About a dozen of our cars were at Trent Park on August Bank Holiday Monday, where the weather was mainly kind to us. It was the last occasion that we could enjoy Dave Norton’s relaxed, informative commentary on the cars as he and Penny are emigrating to Australia. They will be very much missed and Steve Brayshaw from the EDVVS presented them with a carriage clock as a thank you for all they have both done over the years.

For the past couple of years, the Buntingford Show has been an occasion for Len and Sally to top up their suntans. Unfortunately, this year’s event had cloud and a cool wind so apart from an occasional wander up and down the street, we were all in our cars. As Doug and I were driving out, one of the organisers thanked us for coming, saying it was such a pleasure to see our row of E-types every year. A new addition this year was an event programme which featured our E-types on the cover.

I wish that I could take the credit for booking the weather as well as the hotel for the weekend in Norfolk, that seventeen of us enjoyed. It was wall to wall sunshine the whole time. The hotel was comfortable and relaxed with good food and the duty manager, Krystle/Crystal? (she didn’t tell us how she spelt it) made a hit with everyone. Inevitably with seventeen of us we couldn’t stick together all of the time but Swaffam, Collector’s World, Denver Windmill, Norfolk Rural Life Museum, Goodstone Water Gardens and Oxburgh Hall were among the places visited. It was nice to have Tom with us again and Phil and Heather came up from the West Country to join us for the weekend.

A surprisingly small number of us went to Croxley Green show on 17th September; just Alan and Barbara, Brian and Linda, Brian K and ourselves. It was a very enjoyable relaxed day with probably a thousand cars of all descriptions. There were lots of Jaguars, American cars, "funny" cars, everyday cars from the fifties and sixties; a real variety. A C-type and an Aston Martin DBR1 both looked really special even though they were replicas.

The Carter’s Steam Fair with their immaculate lorries and living wagons plus their rare steam powered rides also added to the day’s enjoyment. It was a shame that there wasn’t more of us to enjoy the excellent dodgems.

As a fairly informal, no pre-booking show with voluntary charity donations, it was a worthy addition to the calendar.

Dick received a letter from the organisers of the recent Little Haven Show, thanking us for our £40 donation. Their total had not been finalised at the time of writing but it will finish at over £7,000. Next year’s event will be on 19th August but possibly at a different venue.

Keith W recently passed me an article from The Times regarding Ford’s financial problems and what they may or may not sell. I have included the analysis that was at the end of the article which I still have if anyone is interested.

Scrapping a brand risks incurring a nation's wrath

Kevin Eason - Analysis

Closing down Jaguar would provoke the biggest uproar in British industry for a generation. The meek disposal of Rover was accepted because the company had been a basket case for decades, in its death throes since some of the greatest names in motoring, Morris, Austin, Riley, Triumph were absorbed into an absurd state conglomerate and then hawked around the world to anybody who would take it, but Jaguar rose above all that, regained its independence and put some polish back on the Leaping Cat that has adorned the bonnets of some of the most beautiful cars ever made.

That is why Ford would attract abuse if it even countenanced disposing of a brand that ranks alongside Coca-Cola, the BBC and Rolls-Royce. Jaguar even owned by an American motoring multinational represents everything that is British, a byword for sleek power, fine craftsmanship and elegance. The E-type is a car that still defines a nation four decades after its launch and the new XK sports car springs from that rich heritage.

The problem lies with Ford. It bought the company ridiculously over-priced as part of a gung-ho spending spree, had to spend 20 years trying to update and streamline it and then decided to try to emulate BMW by venturing into the executive sales representatives market. But it did it with a car, the X-type, that was too conservative in styling, too meek in its approach. The result? Apathy among buyers who could see no reason to change from BMW or Audi.

In between, Jaguar was almost the last executive carmaker to introduce diesel engines, the fastest-growing sector anywhere in the marketplace. lan Callum, Jaguar's head of design, says that it takes five years to turn around a car business; the creator of the Aston Martin DB7 and now the new XK sports car needs another five years to return Jaguar to its past glory. Ford has to give Jaguar that time or it will face the wrath of the British nation.

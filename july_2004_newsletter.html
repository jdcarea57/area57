Newsletter - July 2004

XK WEEKEND - June 25th to 27th

The weekend incorporating XK Day was back on the familiar territory of Stamford and Burghley House where the event was held for several years. A sunny Friday morning saw the three XKs leave Dick and Eileen’s. However, we were no further along the A10 than the Hertford area, when Eric flashed his lights at Doug to stop as he had seen Dick pull up in what looked like a cloud of smoke. We headed off to the next junction and came back on ourselves till we saw Dick’s 150 in a lay-by. The smoke had turned out to be steam after a core plug had blown. He arranged to be picked up by the AA and said they would see us at Stamford later with his Daimler Double Six. Eric, John and ourselves went on, stopping for lunch at The Red House, where we are to have breakfast on 18th July. On reaching Stamford we parked in the Lady Anne Hotel car park where Eric and John were staying and then had a wander around the town. At about five p.m. we found that Eileen and Dick had arrived at the B & B where the four of us were staying at the other end of town. It was a lovely old Edwardian house, where the owner had installed a security camera in the car park which we could monitor on our bedroom TV. We met up with Eric and John at a local hostelry for an evening meal. On Saturday morning we joined several other XKs at the Lady Anne to be given our routes to Butterfly Farm not far from Spalding. On the way back to Stamford we visited a Romany Museum to see a large collection of gypsy caravans and watch a video about their history. By this time it was pouring with rain, so Doug and I were a bit soggy.

For the first time the XK dinner at the Lady Anne that evening was a dance as well and after Chairman Brian Davis gave out the various awards we were entertained by a very enjoyable small group and lady singer.

Luckily the sun was shining again on Sunday morning for the short drive to Burghley House for XK Day. I didn’t count how many cars were there but certainly not the number there used to be. After lunch in the Orangery and a bit more socialising with familiar faces it was time to head home with a stop for tea and cake at the Westmill Tea Room.

HODDESDON TOWN CENTRE SHOW - 10th July 2004

Over the years this show has had a remarkably good weather record, but all things change. Plenty of sunshine to start with, but by lunch time the rain had set in. Even so, I don’t think very many cars left early. Our nine cars were joined by the Jaguar engined Allard belonging to friends of ours. It seemed that this year there were more cars and people than the last couple of years have seen. Keith and Audrey were not able to be there this year and extend their usual post show hospitality as it was Laura’s graduation day. Well done Laura and where have the years gone?!

In the SS Register News in the Jaguar Driver it recently mentioned that long summer evenings were the ideal time to drive out to their regular meeting place at Potters Crouch. On July 12th, six of our cars joined Dick, but a lovely summer evening would not be a very accurate description. We were able to sit outside but it was quite a grey evening.

BREAKFAST RUN - 18th July 2004

It seems as though we can’t get enough of Breakfast runs as this was our first mid-season run. Dick had organised it for us at The Red House to the north of Royston. Most people left from Bullsmoor Lane and we had a good clear run, further than Rickling Green but no-one seemed to mind that. After the usual fruit juice and cereals it was help yourselves to all we could eat from what seemed like never ending supplies of bacon, sausages, beans, tomatoes, mushrooms, black pudding, scrambled and fried eggs and fried bread, followed by toast and marmalade and jam. Plentiful refills of coffee and tea were constantly available. I know Dick confirmed numbers with them a couple of days before hand but I think that they must have thought we all had American sized appetites. Unfortunately, it began to rain while we were there so most of the way home was quite wet.

Thanks Dick.

THE REDBRIDGE SHOW - Valentines Park 4th July 2004

Report by Mick & Jacqui Stubbings

This car show was organised by Vicky Blackburn, and we must say that Vicky made getting into the event so easy. Once into the show ground you were directed straight to the spot that had been allocated to you. As soon as the cars were parked out came the seats, the tables, and the drinks. We had just sat down when the unmistakeable smell of bacon being cooked wafted across. Some of us resisted the temptation, others could not.

There was a good showing of Club cars although nobody from Area 57 won a prize. Wayne and Graham were going around helping people to have a good day. We had gone ready for bad weather but although it did cloud over a few times and threatened to rain, it came to nothing. So, carrying on the area 57 tradition, we munched our way through quite a selection of foods, while Len and Sally suffered having to soak up the sunshine.

It was great to meet up and have a pleasant day with fellow members of the Area.

CAPITAL’S FORMULA FOR TOTAL DISASTER

By Dan Fielder (Daily Express)

And now it's live to the first ever London Grand Prix...

Welcome back to the old revolving restaurant of the Telecom Tower, where we're covering this truly historic race. Just give me a moment until the view comes round again and I can tell you what's going on...

And what a race it's been so far, full of thrills, spills and not a few jams. We kicked off to a great fanfare, with the pack revving up on the starting grid outside the Carphone Warehouse at the top of the Hanger Lane Gyratory. Starting positions were decided by lottery, remember, because Ken Livingstone's controversial decision to keep the traffic on the road during the race ruled out a qualifying round. Places on the grid were drawn using Camelot's machine, Guinevere, because it sounds a bit like Genevieve, the much-loved film about another famous car race across London.

More than 250,000 race fans counted down the lights from red to amber to green... and back to red again as the driver of an articulated truck got into an argument with a white van man about who should be the pace car and blocked all three southbound lanes until he got his way. Three hours later and the cars have finally made it to King's Cross. The first pitstop came at Harley Street, where several drivers stopped off for much-needed testosterone injections. Shortly after, it looked as though we were going to have to say goodbye to the Minardis when the impoverished team discovered it couldn't afford the congestion charge. Fortunately, Mayor Livingstone came to the rescue by proposing they join his car-share scheme.

On to Oxford Street, where the cars struggle to fight their way through the thousands of petrolheads crammed in buses, coaches and taxis who are flooding the capital to, er, watch the grand prix. Not to mention the hordes of well-spoken ladies heading for the Debenhams, who can't understand why the sales are so busy. Now look at that! Ralf Schumacher's having a heated debate with a kindly gentleman who's Squeegeed his wind-screen and wants 50p for his trouble. Jenson Button's nipped into Hamleys to pick up some PlayStation games and Juan Pablo Montoya goes off to the Trocadero to try out the PepsiMax ride ("Much scarier than Fl," he admits).

As Rubens Barrichello lands a fine for using the bus lane in an enthralling duel with a cycle rickshaw, marshals speed things up and divert the race through Old Holborn and along the Strand to Pall Mall. Is there nothing Bemie Ecclestone won't try to get round the ban on tobacco advertising? Into the Mall, on goes the power and through the gears they go/ First! Second! Third! And first again, as Takuma Sato cuts a corner across St James's Park, sending deckchairs flying but swerving to avoid a flock of flinty-eyed pigeons.

As the field rounds Buckingham Comer, Button pulls an outrageous left-hand doughnut to salute Her Majesty the Queen up on the balcony (who's still furious that David Beckham hasn't won Wimbledon yet). Through the Victoria chicane, dropping to 27mph as they go past New Scotland Yard, the first cars are then lapped as Big Ben sweeps into view. Oh yes, it's stragglers Christian Klien and Mark Webber, much to the annoyance of Deputy PM John Prescott, who had big money on the two Jags.

Hard on the brakes as they round Parliament Square, the drivers cross the Thames and pop in for a tyre change at the Kwik-Fit in Vauxhall, head on to Battersea Bridge, up King's Road and through Sloane Square. Jamo Trulli is disqualified when an official spots him sneaking onto the Circle Line, while several of these fashion-conscious millionaires opt for a pit stop in Prada. But permit parking means we lose both Jordans and a Sauber to some aggressive clamping.

Now, to the roar of the grandstand, Michael Schumacher leads the remaining cars round Hyde Park Corner and flat out into Park Lane at a mind-blistering 35mph. BMW, Mercedes and the other luxury car showrooms have never seen so much custom in a single day. Regrettably, most of the people wanting test drives are pasty-skinned IT consultants dressed head to toe in the faded Ferrari team colours. Down they come to Marble Arch, past Speaker's Comer, and all they have to do to complete the circuit is nip down Lancaster Gate, fight through Netting Hill, down Holland Park, round the Thames Water Tower roundabout, edge down the A41M slip road, take a left on to the A40... from there it's just a three-hour crawl back to Hanger Lane.

It may not be your average grand prix but that's driving in London for you. Only another 71 laps to go...

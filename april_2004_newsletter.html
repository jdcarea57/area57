Although the May Newsletter will probably be with you before the Enfield Pageant over the Spring Bank Holiday weekend, I thought I would do the usual reminders now, just in case. It may seem rather early in the year to talk about Club Subscriptions but from next year we will be making a slight change. Renewal forms are sent out with the December Newsletter, and anyone who doesn’t return this is then sent a final reminder with the January Newsletter. During February the new membership lists are compiled, so receiving renewals after this causes delay and additional work. In order to discourage late payment, it has therefore been decided that renewals received after the end of February will incur an additional £5.00 rejoining fee.

A REDDITCH RENDEZVOUS WITH EDVVS - (Friday 16th-Sunday 18th April)

Report by Audrey & Keith Williams

Friday

A beautiful sunny morning – HOOD DOWN – ready for a leisurely drive through the Cotswolds. We had carefully planned our route with the aid of ‘The Good Pub Guide 2004’, and had selected a National Trust hostelry, ‘The Fleece’ , at Bretforton, near Evesham for lunch. When we arrived shock, horror ! the place was closed for renovation !!. Luckily we found a pub in the next village which served the purpose.

We met up with the rest of the party from Whitewebbs for dinner at the Abbey Hotel (Best Western). Afterwards we really had to concentrate, as the now famous compulsory quiz was distributed. This time the theme was based on Cinema films, the cryptic clues for which were somewhat challenging to say the least ! We’ll use some of the easier questions for our Club trip in September !!

Saturday

Another sunny morning – HOOD DOWN again- to join the convoy of 45 different vehicles (ours being surprisingly the only E-type – makes a change!) to the Black Country Open Air Museum ,near Dudley. Although we had visited it before, there was still plenty to see and do. Our favourites were the trip down the coal mine and the ride on the canal narrow boat underground where they used to transport the coal & limestone . All the trips incidentally being included in the price of the weekend – brilliant value.

Saturday night was ‘The Mad Hatters Party’. We cheated and wore two ‘Castaway’ Straw Hats out of Laura’s old fancy-dress collection – but some people had gone to a lot of trouble making their own creations. There was a forfeit to be paid for anyone not wearing a hat to dinner !

Sunday

A wet , windy morning – HOOD definitely up! – to join the rally to Cadbury’s World at Bournville only some 8-10 miles away. Again we had visited it before some 12 years ago so were not particularly enthusiastic. Unfortunately, they have made many changes and it is now a sort of childrens Theme Park with rides, clowns, videos and amusement arcades. Not much of the old factory or the chocolate-making process to be seen. Still. Laura enjoyed all the free samples we had collected on the way round !

We returned to the hotel for Sunday lunch and presentations. Sadly, Stan Chamberlaine was awarded the Breakdown Trophy ( Piston Broke) as his S-type Jag had to be trailered home to Radlett from the Black Country Museum, as his power-steering unit had completely packed up.

We left Redditch at just before 4pm in torrential run and apart from getting wet knees from the inevitable leaks from the hood we arrived home safely just after 6pm, after another very enjoyable weekend, with very friendly people. Several members from the EDVVS again told us they would welcome more Jag Club people to come along.

Once again we pass on the open invitation to a further weekend in October?

JAGUAR XJ - First Impressions of the seventh generation of Jaguar’s big saloon

From Motoring & Leisure (by Peter Cracknell)

We don't see bad new cars any more, just varying shades of good ones, and every once in a while one that is exceptional, like the new Jaguar XJ - seventh in a long line of big saloons that started life in 1968 with the code name experimental Jaguar.

To maximise the appeal of the new model, Jaguar apparently focused on style and driving dynamics. If part of style is the impression the car gives you on first seeing it, then improvements here seem to have been less than revolutionary. However, Jaguar were mindful of the fact that focus groups of existing customers had praised the design as it was - they didn't see the need for change - so substantial revisions to the outline were a no-no. But under the skin there are changes aplenty: for a start, that skin is now made of aluminium. Obvious advantages of this 200kg weight saving are improved performance and economy, but for the keen driver there's the added bonus of a more nimble car, one that feels smaller than it actually is. Ally this to new air suspension, and the big family saloon could, with the right engines, become a driver's delight.

Any of the four engines on offer - a 3.0-litre V6, plus V8s of 3.5, 4.2 and 4.2 supercharged - ensures that it does. The inclusion of a V6 enables a lead-in price not too far removed from the base of the old range. It's a very smooth performer with ample performance to enable one to enjoy the car's amazing ride and handling. The suspension system of all models uses Jaguar's Computer Active Technology Suspension which adapts ride firmness and height to match the manner in which the car is being driven. SE and Sport variants do have slightly different initial settings, but none is harsh, and the changes that CATS makes as one takes tight bends or drives gently in a straight line are imperceptible; just like the gear changes. Six-speed auto transmission is standard, and the only way you can tell that the system has changed gear is to look at the rev counter; you can't normally hear the engine, and you can't feel the change.

The V8 engines are naturally even smoother than the V6, and culminate in the supercharged 4.2 which we first reported on when it appeared in the S-type R last year. Despite being a larger car than the S-type, the aluminium XJ drives, handles and performs like a true sports car, yet the ride is totally comfortable. It is, I assure you, a memorable experience, but you'll tell me that with prices running from 39 to over 68 thousand pounds, it should be!

With a length of over five metres it's no surprise that legroom is generous all round. Boot space is improved over the old model, and now measures 470 litres (16.6 cubic feet), though it's still a fairly shallow compartment. All models feature front, side and curtain airbags, ABS, emergency brake assist, plus automatic handbrake, headlights and wipers, and electric operation of almost everything.

STORY OF A TRUE CLASSIC

Daily Express - Saturday 10th April

Is the Jaguar E-Type the most beutiful car of all time? Elegant, glamorous, and surprisingly good value for money, there’s a lot to be said for the car that was unveiled to an astounded public at the 1961 Geneva Motor Show.

The good news is that you’ll be able to take a step back in time if you take a trip to London this summer. From August 1st an exhibition, The E-Type - Story Of A British Sports Car, which celebrates the Coventry firm’s favourite coupe and one of the main cultural icons of the Sixties, will be on show at the Design Museum. Featuring memorabilia and rare cars from the Jaguar Daimler Heritage Trust, the exhibition will trace the evolution of the E-Type from Jaguar’s XK120, C-Type and D-Types, which won the 24-hour races at Le Mans five times in the Fifties, as well as details of the early development of the 150mph E-Type under the supervision of the marque’s founder, William Lyons.

What is often forgotten about the E-Type is just what good value it was. At just £2,097 for its roadster model, it was half the price of a Ferrari or an Aston Martin that had similar performance. The museum, in Shad Thames near Tower Bridge, will be running the E-Type exhibition until November 28th, with adult tickets costing £6. Further details are available by phone on 0870-833 9955 or on website www.designmuseum.org

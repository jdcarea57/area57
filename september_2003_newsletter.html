Newsletter - September 2003
 
As we come to the end of a glorious summer, we now only have the Breakfast Run on 5th October left. Let’s hope the weather will behave on that day as well.
 
It would have been almost impossible for the weather on August Bank Holiday Monday to be worse than it was last year. As it turned out, it wasn’t as hot as we’d had before, or since, but pleasant enough for a lazy day. Anybody looking for a big car show would probably have gone to Knebworth but for a relaxing day that includes cars, steam, vintage tractors and heavy horses this show, now in it’s third year, can’t be beaten. The small fairground included dodgems which act like a magnet to Area 57. I think we had about ten cars for three rides. The last one definitely seemed shorter, so maybe they were trying to tell us something! On our way out, Doug and I met Dave Norton of the EDVVS, who expressed his gratitude for the way in which the Area supports Museum events.
 
CRICKLADE WEEKEND 12th -14th September

I wish I could take credit for the perfect weather that we enjoyed for the complete weekend; hardly a cloud in the sky.

A mini convoy left London, consisting of our E-type, Dick Rogers' and Eric Tetsell’s XK150s and Dick’s Daimler Double 6 which Keith Oswick had borrowed due to a problem with his Daimler 250. I had circled a few places on my map which were possibilities for lunch after browsing through an AA pub guide. As time went on, two were looking likely and we finally settled on the first of these. We drove into the car park to find two E-types already there and Len & Sally Smerald and Keith & Audrey Williams tucking into lunch. They had chosen it from another book and we had no idea they would be there. We headed off a little later than they did and were quite surprised to find them join on our tail not far from the hotel. Having taken a more main road than us, they had got caught up in the aftermath of an accident. Phil and Heather Kendon had got to the hotel a short while before us and it wasn’t long before we were sitting on a lovely terrace enjoying tea.

On Saturday morning we visited Snowshill Manor in the northern part of the Cotswolds. This houses a collection of thousands of items including model Samurai warriors, clocks, musical instruments, bikes, prams, spinning wheels, dolls houses and goodness knows what else. A visit to a nearby steam railway was abandoned in favour of a lazy afternoon back at the hotel. Phil and Heather left shortly after breakfast the next morning as they were going on to their daughter’s at Bristol. The rest of us drove to the lovely village of Bilbury which, like so many of the Cotswold villages, is a photographer’s dream. We then carried on to Burford for lunch and a wander up and down the main street before saying our goodbyes. We were all very impressed with the Cricklade Hotel; the service and food were excellent. Nothing was too much trouble and they were quite happy to find us a room to ourselves for coffee, while we attempted Keith and Audrey’s quiz. This proved to be a bit easier than the one Dick and Eileen set us on Friday evening which had us all racking our brains.

BUNTINGFORD CLASSIC SHOW and QUIZ NIGHT - Saturday 6th September

Report by Audrey Williams

I joined our group at lunchtime and was surprised to see how little space had been allocated for us. The parking was very tight and there was no room to "set up camp" with our usual tables and chairs. It really was standing room only!!! - but overall a very good show. Alan Colllins estimated there were 158+ cars, to say nothing of a large amount of motorcycles. There was a wide variety of vehicles, ranging from a modern Williams Renault Spyder to an early 30’s Austin Cambridge. The car voted as Best in Show by exhibitors was a ‘concours’ Jensen Interceptor (which has been seen at many other shows).

Buntingford is a pretty town - quite olde worlde. Bargain of the day was the home made cakes served with tea or coffee at St. John’s Church - all for £1. Everyone in our club became quite frustrated with the showers as they had to leather the cars down 3 or 4 times during the day!!

Most of us met up again during the evening to support the Quiz Night at Martin Rogers’ School. Eileen and her colleagues had spent a lot of time and effort organising the evening. We all found the TV theme tune round quite difficult!! Fish and chips plus soft drinks were included in the £5 admission fee (they could have easily doubled the price). Eileen had made huge strawberry and raspberry pavlovas as an extra. Our club did very well on the raffle. Most of us won a prize!! We all felt the Quiz Night was wonderful value for money. Let’s hope with the raffle the proceeds will have boosted school funds for a few extras.

Thank you Eileen for your hard work. (I gather about £200 profit was made.)

Tracks of wife’s tears

Builder Steve Whitehouse sold his wife’s £50,000 Jaguar XK8 to buy a Second World War tank. Mr Whitehouse, 37, saw the £33,000 American M24 Chaffee tank at a military show in Belgium. He said yesterday: "I made the deal there and then. I was fretting all the way home about how to tell the wife - I’d already shaken hands on the deal."

Mr Whitehouse, of Frome Somerset, will register the 1944 tank so he can drive it on roads but will mainly use it at shows. Wife Dawn, 27, said of her XK8: "It was the car of my dreams. We saved up for ages to buy it and I was very upset. But we have a baby girl now, Mia, so it would not have been practical."

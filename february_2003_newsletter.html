MORE NUMBERPLATE PROBLEMS? - Owners of certain classic cars could face more uncertainty about numberplates if DVLA proposals to introduce electronic plates are made law.

From Classic Car Weekly

Another threat to classic number plates may be on the way in 2004, with a proposal to introduce electronic numberplates which can be recognised by beacons and cameras by the roadside, and by 2007, if the plans come to fruition, every vehicle in Britain will have to have them.

The DVLA is exploring ways that security can be improved on numberplates, and one option is that plates will have a microchip containing vehicle details such as make, colour, engine, registered keeper, MoT status and insurance. If introduced, new plates will have to be fitted to over 29-million vehicles, including many classics. The fear among classic owners is that old style plates with white/silver lettering on a black metal background won't be manufactured with electronic tags on them, and all cars will have to sport modern plates.

"My concern is that the DVLA will, I am sure, insist on these plates being the yellow/white with black lettering type, as used by modern cars, with, no doubt, the additional indignity for classic cars of the blue EU flag," said Classic Car Weekly reader Hugh Taberner.

A question mark also hangs over how the system would be implemented on classics with stick-on numberplates, like the Jaguar E-type. However, the DVLA is remaining traditionally tight-lipped about the plans. A spokesman told CCW, "The DVLA is currently exploring a number of options for making number plates more secure - electronic tagging is merely one of those options. Recent articles in the media are a gross exaggeration of the current position. A consultation exercise would be undertaken prior to the introduction of any changes."

The possible move would be the latest 'Big Brother' measure aimed at motorists, although the electronic plates could dramatically cut down on the number of vehicle thefts. Police cars are also likely to have the numberplate readers, and any false plate spotted will alert the police national computer and the DVLA. The beacons and cameras will also be able to search for vehicles reported stolen.

This latest news marks the end of a bad year for classic number plate manufacturers. Earlier in 2002, CCW revealed the news that from January 1, 2003, plate manufacturers will have to be registered and record identity details of both vehicle and purchaser of any replacement plates. As those making plates will have to have sight of original documents, this is likely to create serious problems for those manufacturing pre-1973-style plates, with the majority of their sales coming via mail order.

Only last week, the hazards of letting important personal documents out of owners' hands was highlighted by the BBC when a reporter managed to obtain a provisional driving licence in the name of home secretary David Blunkett by using a copy of his birth certificate. The hijacking of people's details by criminals has tripled in the past two years, with fake identities being used to commit fraud and aid illegal immigrants to enter and stay in Britain.

There is a shred of good news for beleagured mail order numberplate manufacturers though. Although the requirement for companies to register with the DVLA came in as planned at the beginning of January, prosecutions for those who don't will not be introduced for three months.
